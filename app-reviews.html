Cryptoasset Promotions
App Review Compliance

A practical compliance guide for preparing in-app financial promotions for submission and approval. Covers all screens accessible to UK users — from login through to trading and product flows.

⬥ Zeyro reviews every screen accessible to UK users — including pre-login, onboarding, trading, and product flows
⬥ Submissions are typically made via Figma file or PDF flow export
⬥ This guide applies to all screens, not just the home page or sign-up flow
🌎 A Note on Identifying UK Users
The FCA financial promotion regime applies to promotions directed at UK users. The primary method for identifying UK users should be geolocation via IP detection. Citizenship alone is less reliable — a UK citizen living abroad would typically be subject to the regulations of their country of residence. Firms must have a clear, documented mechanism for identifying and applying UK regulatory requirements to the correct user cohort.

What Constitutes an App Financial Promotion
FSMA 2000 s.21 / COBS 4.12A
▾

A financial promotion is any communication that is an invitation or inducement to engage in investment activity. In the context of a crypto app, this applies broadly — it is not limited to dedicated marketing screens.

  • Pre-login and sign-up screens — any headline, strapline, or feature description visible before a user is authenticated
  • Login screens — benefit claims or product descriptions displayed on the login page
  • Home page / dashboard — asset listings, rate quotes, feature cards, and promotional banners
  • Asset listing pages — individual token pages showing price, description, and buy/sell options
  • Product screens — staking, earn, borrow, swap, or any yield-generating feature
  • Account centre and tier/benefit pages — where membership benefits or features are promoted
  • Transactional review screens — where asset risks must be present before confirmation
  • Push notifications and in-app messages — where submitted as part of the approval (separate ticket required)
Screens That Are Not Financial Promotions

Pure utility screens with no promotional content — such as KYC document upload screens, settings menus, or transaction history pages — are generally not financial promotions provided they contain no benefit claims, rate quotes, or product descriptions. If in doubt, include them in your submission; Zeyro will confirm their status.

The Risk Warning Banner
COBS 4.12A.10R / COBS 4.12A.11R / COBS 4.12A.39
▾
Prescribed Wording — COBS 4.12A.11R
"Don't invest unless you're prepared to lose all the money you invest. This is a high-risk investment and you should not expect to be protected if something goes wrong. Take 2 mins to learn more"

The "Take 2 mins to learn more" link must direct users to a risk summary page following the structure in COBS 4 Annex 1R. The link text must be exact — do not rephrase it.

Placement

The banner must appear at the top of each app screen that is a financial promotion — below any fixed navigation bar but above all other content. It must not scroll off the screen as the user scrolls down. Confirm in your submission that the banner remains fixed and visible throughout the scroll journey.

App-Specific Prominence Issues

In-app environments introduce specific design challenges that frequently lead to compliance failures. The following are the most common issues flagged during app reviews:

✓ Compliant✗ Non-Compliant
✓Risk warning banner in a clearly defined, distinctly coloured band that contrasts with the surrounding UI✗Grey banner on a grey or dark background — this fails the prominence requirement even where grey is a brand colour
✓Banner text in a legible font size matching or exceeding the standard body text used elsewhere in the app✗A coloured UI element placed directly above the banner that reduces the visual separation between the banner and the content above it
✓Banner fixed at the top of the screen and visible as the user scrolls through the page✗Banner visible only at the top of the initial screen load, scrolling off as the user moves down
✓Banner applied to all screens that constitute financial promotions, including post-login screens, asset listings, and product pages✗Banner present on sign-up and pre-login screens only, absent from home page or trading screens
✓Shaded border or background applied to the risk warning to increase visual separation from surrounding content✗Banner displayed without sufficient contrast — blending into the app's dark-mode or brand colour scheme
Real Example from Our Reviews
One app used a grey banner on a dark-themed interface, arguing it was consistent with brand guidelines. This does not satisfy the FCA's prominence requirement under COBS 4.12A.39 — the banner must stand out from the surrounding content, regardless of brand colour preferences. The issue required remediation before approval.
Real Example from Our Reviews
Another submission placed a brightly coloured UI element directly above the risk warning banner, reducing the visual separation between the two. We recommended adjusting the colour of that element to provide clearer visual separation and ensure the risk warning remained sufficiently prominent.
3
The Approval Time Stamp
COBS 4.10.7A
▾

Under COBS 4.10.7A, every financial promotion must display the name of the approving firm and the date of approval with sufficient prominence. For app screens, this stamp must appear on every screen that contains the risk warning banner.

Prescribed Wording
"This Financial Promotion has been approved by Zeyro LTD (FRN 1001386) on [DATE]"

Replace [DATE] with the date on which Zeyro provided written approval. Do not use a placeholder or leave the field blank when publishing. The wording must refer specifically to "financial promotions" — not "content", "offers", or any other term.

Placement in App Screens

The approval time stamp is typically placed at the bottom of each app screen, or within a visible footer element, below the main content. It must be clearly legible — not in a font size smaller than the surrounding text, and not in a colour that blends with the background.

Where an app is submitted via Figma, the time stamp should be added to all relevant screens in the final approved flow before publication. Screens submitted for review without the time stamp will require it to be added before the promotion goes live.

Updating After Changes
If your app content changes after initial approval, you must resubmit the updated screens. Where changes are minor and within the scope of the originally approved content, Zeyro will confirm on a case-by-case basis whether the original approval date can be retained. Do not update the live time stamp date without written confirmation from Zeyro.
4
Balancing Language
COBS 4.2.1R
▾

Every benefit claim, feature description, or rate quote in your app must be accompanied by appropriately balanced risk disclosure. The general risk warning banner alone does not satisfy this requirement — specific, contextualised risk language must appear alongside the relevant content throughout the body of your app screens.

The key question to ask for each screen: for every benefit described here, is there a corresponding risk the user needs to understand? That risk information must appear in proximity to the benefit — not only in the footer, the risk summary, or a separate page.

Core Principles
  • Risk disclosures must be presented with equal prominence to the benefits they accompany — this means matching font size, weight, and visibility
  • Risk information must not be hidden, de-emphasised, or grouped at the bottom of a screen where a user might not see it
  • Where benefits are described using bold text or large font, the accompanying risk language must be equally visible — not in small grey text beneath
  • Risk language must be specific to the feature being described — generic disclaimers are not sufficient
Placement

Risk disclosures must appear in proximity to the relevant feature or claim — not only in the footer or on a separate page. Where a benefit is promoted in a card or module within the app, the risk disclaimer should be immediately adjacent or directly beneath that element.

Risk language placed in a hover tooltip or behind a "learn more" link is generally not acceptable as the primary disclosure. Where an asterisk (*) is used to flag a footnote, the associated risk text must appear on the same screen — not only in the terms and conditions.

✓ Compliant✗ Non-Compliant
✓"Earn up to 15% APY on select tokens. Rate variable and subject to change. APY is not guaranteed and depends on market conditions. You could lose money."✗"Earn up to 15% APY on select tokens." — risk disclaimer buried in footer in small grey text
✓Risk language displayed in the same font size and weight as benefit text, directly beneath the feature description✗Risk language displayed in a small font beneath a large headline, making it visually subordinate to the benefit claim
✓APY asterisk footnote visible on the same screen as the rate figure✗APY asterisk with footnote explanation only accessible via a link to terms and conditions

Asset-Specific Risks
COBS 4.2.1R / COBS 4.12A
▾

Where your app refers to or promotes specific types of cryptoasset, you must include risk disclosures tailored to that asset type. A general risk warning is not sufficient where specific assets are being promoted. The risks must be fully visible on the relevant screen — they cannot be hidden behind expand buttons, "read more" links, or separate pages.

Baseline Risks — Applicable to All Cryptoassets
RiskDescription
Investment riskThe value of cryptoassets can be extremely volatile. Prices may fall just as quickly as they rise, and you could lose all of the money you invest.
Lack of protectionsCryptoassets are largely unregulated. You will not be protected by the FSCS or the FOS if something goes wrong.
Selling your investmentYou may not always be able to sell your cryptoassets when you want. Market conditions or operational factors may affect your ability to exit.
Cryptoassets are complexIt may not always be clear how a cryptoasset works or what factors influence its value. Take time to understand the product before investing.
DiversificationIt is good practice not to invest more than 10% of your money in high-risk investments such as cryptoassets.
DeFi Token Risks
RiskDescription
Smart contract riskDeFi relies on smart contracts. Even small coding errors or bugs can lead to exploits, potentially resulting in the loss of all invested funds.
Regulatory riskDeFi protocols often operate without intermediaries or standard compliance controls. Future regulation may restrict access or significantly affect the value of DeFi tokens.
Rug pulls and exit scamsSome DeFi projects are launched by anonymous teams, increasing the risk of developers abandoning or fraudulently exiting a project with investor funds.
Data / oracle riskDeFi platforms depend on external data sources for pricing and execution. If these are manipulated or fail, the platform may behave in unexpected ways.
Protocol complexityMany DeFi systems are difficult to understand even for experienced investors. You should not invest in DeFi products you do not fully understand.
Memecoin Risks
RiskDescription
Volatility riskMemecoins often experience extreme and unpredictable price swings, heavily influenced by social media trends and celebrity endorsements.
Lack of utilityMemecoins often lack intrinsic value and utility, relying on community interest and online trends to sustain their value.
Market manipulationMemecoins are susceptible to "pump-and-dump" schemes and coordinated price manipulation.
Community-driven risksThese tokens rely on community participation without formal governance structures. If community interest declines, the value may fall rapidly.
Emotional investingMemecoins attract strong emotional responses and viral excitement, which can lead to impulsive or irrational investment decisions.
Stablecoin Risks
RiskDescription
Counterparty riskIf the stablecoin is backed by reserves, a third party is responsible for holding and managing those reserves. If they fail or act improperly, the peg may collapse.
Redemption riskThe ability to redeem an asset for its underlying collateral may not function as expected, especially during periods of market stress or high demand.
Collateral riskSome stablecoins are backed by other cryptoassets. If the value of the collateral falls significantly, the stablecoin may lose its peg.
Foreign exchange (FX) riskMany stablecoins are pegged to fiat currencies, exposing you to movements in exchange rates relative to your home currency.
Algorithmic riskStablecoins that rely on algorithms or smart contracts to maintain their peg can fail due to flawed design or sudden market conditions.
Wrapped Token Risks
RiskDescription
Smart contract riskWrapped tokens depend on smart contracts to maintain their link to the underlying asset. If the contract is exploited or has errors, you could lose your funds.
Collateral riskThe value of a wrapped token is typically backed by an equivalent amount of the underlying asset. If the backing mechanism fails, the wrapped token may lose its value.
Custodial riskMany wrapped tokens rely on a third-party custodian to hold the original asset. If this custodian is hacked, becomes insolvent, or acts improperly, the wrapped token may become worthless.
Bridging riskWrapped tokens are often created through cross-chain bridges. These bridges can be vulnerable to technical failures or exploits.
Price divergence riskAlthough designed to mirror the value of the original asset, the wrapped token may trade at a premium or discount during periods of market stress.
Security Token Risks
RiskDescription
Regulatory riskSecurity tokens may fall under financial regulations. Offering, holding, or trading them without proper authorisation may constitute a breach of law in certain jurisdictions.
Issuer riskThe value of a security token is tied to a specific company, project, or asset. If the issuer underperforms or fails, the token's value may fall to zero.
Liquidity riskSecurity tokens often trade on a limited number of platforms, making it difficult to sell your tokens when you want to.
Legal complexityRights attached to security tokens may be hard to enforce, particularly across different jurisdictions.
Custody and transferability riskSecurity tokens may require specific custody solutions and may not be supported across all wallets or exchanges.
Governance & Utility Token Risks
RiskDescription
Project dependencyThese tokens are closely tied to the ongoing development and success of a specific platform or protocol. If the project fails, the token may become worthless.
Governance concentrationVoting rights may be concentrated among a small number of holders, reducing the effectiveness of decentralised governance.
Volatility riskEven if designed for use within a platform, these tokens can experience significant price swings driven by speculation and market sentiment.
Functionality riskIf the platform or protocol changes its structure, the utility of the token may diminish significantly.
Regulatory uncertaintyThe regulatory treatment of governance and utility tokens is still evolving, which may affect their legality or value in certain jurisdictions.

Service / Product-Specific Risks
COBS 4.2.1R / COBS 4.12A
▾

Where your app promotes specific products or services, you must include risk disclosures tailored to that feature. A general risk warning about cryptoassets is not sufficient where specific services are being highlighted. The risks below must appear alongside the relevant product screen — not only in the terms and conditions or a linked page.

Staking
RiskDescription
Price volatility while stakedThe value of your staked assets can fall significantly during the staking period.
Inability to sell during unstakingYou may not be able to access or sell your assets until the unstaking process is complete.
Length of unstaking periodExplain the specific unstaking or lock-up period applicable to your product, including the process and timeline for accessing funds after unstaking. Confirm whether any user action is required at the end of the lock-up period.
Loss riskRewards are not guaranteed and you may receive less than expected, or lose part of your staked capital.
Crypto Lending
RiskDescription
Counterparty riskThe borrower or platform may default, resulting in partial or total loss of the lent assets.
Liquidity riskYou may not be able to access your funds during the lending period.
Smart contract riskWhere lending is facilitated by a smart contract, bugs or exploits could result in loss of funds.
Rate variabilityInterest rates on crypto lending are variable and may change significantly over time.
Collateral riskIf collateral provided by the borrower falls in value, your lent assets may not be fully recoverable.
Crypto Borrowing
RiskDescription
Liquidation riskIf the value of your collateral falls below a required threshold, your position may be automatically liquidated, resulting in loss of your collateral.
Interest rate riskBorrowing rates may fluctuate. Rising rates can significantly increase the cost of your loan.
Collateral volatilityThe collateral you provide is typically a cryptoasset and may fall in value, increasing the risk of liquidation.
Repayment riskFailure to repay your loan or maintain the required collateral level may result in the loss of your deposited assets.
Platform riskThe platform facilitating the loan may experience technical issues, insolvency, or regulatory action.
OTC Trading Services
RiskDescription
Counterparty riskOTC trades are executed directly with a counterparty. If the counterparty defaults or acts fraudulently, you may lose your funds.
Price riskOTC prices may differ from market prices. There is no guarantee that the rate quoted will be favourable or competitive at the time of execution.
Liquidity riskOTC desks rely on their own liquidity sources. During periods of market stress, liquidity may be limited and trade execution may be delayed or unavailable.
Settlement riskThere is a risk that a trade is not settled as agreed, particularly in cross-border or cross-currency transactions.
Regulatory riskOTC services may be subject to regulatory requirements in multiple jurisdictions. Ensure you understand the regulatory context in which your OTC provider operates.
APY / Yield Figures
  • State clearly that rates are subject to change and are not guaranteed
  • Explain how the APY or yield is calculated and what assumptions it is based on
  • Include a source and effective date for any rate quoted
  • Where rates are promotional or introductory, clearly state the duration and end conditions
  • Include a disclaimer that past rates are not indicative of future returns
  • Where an asterisk is used to flag a footnote, the associated explanation must be visible on the same screen — not only in the terms and conditions
Past Performance Charts
  • Include a prominent disclaimer that past performance is not a reliable indicator of future results
  • Charts must default to showing the "All Time" or "MAX" view — do not pre-select a view that emphasises short-term positive performance
  • Include the source of the data displayed

Sensitive Words & Misleading Claims
COBS 4.2.1R / COBS 4.2.5G
▾

App copy is often written with conversion and engagement in mind — short, punchy, benefit-led. This creates a high frequency of compliance issues in practice. The following phrases appear repeatedly in app review feedback and should be addressed before submission.

Problematic PhraseWhy It Is FlaggedSuggested Approach
✗ "Buy crypto in seconds" / "instantly" / "in a flash"Implies speed and simplicity in a way that minimises the consideration and risk involved in purchasing high-risk investments.✓ Remove time-implying language. Replace with neutral descriptions: "Browse available assets" or "Build your crypto portfolio".
✗ "Earn effortlessly" / "Watch rewards roll in automatically"Implies passive, risk-free profits. "Effortlessly" downplays the risk and complexity of yield products. "Automatically" implies certainty of return.✓ "View rewards earned from eligible assets — returns may vary and are not guaranteed."
✗ "Safe" / "Securely" / "Secure your crypto"Sensitive words under COBS 4.2.5G. Imply capital protection that does not exist. Cryptoassets are not covered by the FSCS.✓ Remove or replace with factual descriptions of specific security measures: "We use multi-factor authentication and cold storage. Cryptoassets are not covered by the FSCS."
✗ "Simple" / "Easy" / "Hassle-free"Downplays the complexity and risk of cryptoasset investing.✓ Remove. Focus on what is well-structured or efficient, with appropriate qualification.
✗ "Enjoy full protection"Directly implies FSCS or regulatory protection, which does not apply to cryptoassets for UK users.✓ Remove. If referring to specific technical safeguards, describe them specifically and include a clear FSCS disclaimer.
✗ Unqualified award claimsAward claims require substantiation under COBS 4.2.1R. Vague references without specificity are high risk.✓ Include the full award name, awarding body, and year.
✗ "Up to X% yearly" without qualificationWhere staking periods are shorter than a year, annualised rate language may not reflect what users actually receive. "Up to" without context is vague and potentially misleading.✓ "You may receive staking rewards of up to the equivalent of X% per year, shown as an annualised rate. Rewards are variable, dynamic, and not guaranteed. Your capital remains at risk."
The "Show, Don't Tell" Principle
Rather than labelling your product as "secure", "trusted", or "protected", describe specifically what processes, safeguards, or standards you apply. This approach is both more credible and more compliant. Let users draw their own conclusions from the facts you provide.

Referral Programmes & Incentives
COBS 4.12A.7R
▾

Under COBS 4.12A.7R, firms are prohibited from offering monetary or non-monetary incentives to UK users as an inducement to engage with, sign up to, or invest in a qualifying cryptoasset. This is an absolute restriction — it applies regardless of how the incentive is structured or framed.

Prohibited Incentives
  • Refer-a-friend reward schemes — offering cash, tokens, or any reward to a user for introducing a new investor. Even where the reward is modest, this is prohibited where it is tied to the referred user engaging with or investing in a cryptoasset product. This prohibition applies whether the reward goes to the referrer, the referred user, or both
  • Sign-up bonuses — offering a financial reward in exchange for registering or completing onboarding
  • Rewards tied to trading volume — for example, offering cashback, bonus tokens, or enhanced rates contingent on a user reaching a trading threshold or activity level
  • Limited-time offers — time-limited promotions designed to encourage investment within a specific window, as these create artificial urgency that induces investment activity
  • Tiered loyalty rewards — where reaching a higher membership or account tier unlocks investment-related rewards, thereby incentivising users to invest more to qualify
Referral Codes

Where an app includes a referral code field during sign-up, the use of referral codes to identify users arriving via partner or affiliate channels is generally permissible on its own. However, if the referral code is linked to any form of benefit, reward, or preferential rate for either the referrer or the referred user, this will constitute a prohibited incentive under COBS 4.12A.7R.

Before including a referral code field in your submission, confirm what specific benefit (if any) is associated with the code and provide this detail in your submission. Zeyro cannot approve a referral mechanism without understanding what reward it triggers.

Engagement Tokens and Non-Cash Rewards

Where a firm issues non-tradable, non-transferable engagement tokens awarded purely for engagement (not linked to investment activity), these may fall outside the prohibition. However, if such tokens are visible on the same screen as tradable cryptoassets, care must be taken to ensure the screen as a whole is not misleading. Confirm the nature of these tokens clearly in your submission.

Customer Journey Accuracy
COBS 4.2.1R
▾
Representing the Onboarding Journey Accurately

Your app must accurately represent the journey a UK user will go through before they can access trading functionality. Language that implies immediate or near-instant access to trading is misleading because UK users must complete several mandatory steps before they can trade. Where post-onboarding screens include copy referencing immediate trading, this must also be reviewed and amended.

Substantiating Claims

Any claim made about your product or service within the app must be capable of being evidenced. This includes comparative claims (e.g. "faster", "lower fees"), superlative claims (e.g. "the most cost-effective"), and outcome-based claims (e.g. "save money", "grow your assets").

  • If you cannot evidence a claim, remove or rephrase it
  • Comparative claims must specify what they are being compared to and include supporting evidence
  • Superlative claims ("best", "most", "leading") are high risk — they will almost always require substantiation or removal
  • Outcome-based claims imply certainty and must be qualified with appropriate risk language
Regulatory Framing

Many firms are regulated or licensed in other jurisdictions. References to this regulatory status within the app can create a false impression that UK regulatory protections apply. Any reference to your regulatory status must be accompanied by a clear clarification that this does not provide UK protections, and this clarification must appear with equal prominence to the original claim.

Recommended Clarification
"We are not a UK-authorised firm, so UK users cannot refer complaints to the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS), and investments are not covered by the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS)."

The Back-End Frictions Flow
COBS 4.12A.18R–4.12A.27G / COBS 10A
▾

For most app submissions, a significant proportion of the review feedback relates to the back-end frictions flow — the mandatory sequence of safeguards that UK users must complete before they can access trading functionality. This includes:

  • The Personalised Risk Warning — delivered using the user's full name, presented with equal-prominence Leave/Continue options
  • Client Categorisation — self-categorisation as a High Net-Worth Investor or Restricted Investor using the verbatim FCA annex statements
  • Appropriateness Assessment — a minimum of 10 questions per set, covering all topics in COBS 10 Annex 4G, with a recommended 80% pass rate and increasing lockout periods after failed attempts
  • 24-Hour Cooling-Off Period — with a visible countdown and an equally prominent Leave/Continue screen presented when the period concludes
