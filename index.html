Back-End Frictions Compliance

"Back-end frictions" are deliberate friction points or safeguards built into the customer journey after a user has seen a financial promotion, but before they are allowed to engage with a high-risk investment (like cryptoassets). These frictions are designed to slow down impulsive action, ensure the client is suitably informed, and that they meet the regulatory criteria for investing. This guide provides a breakdown of what that includes under FCA rules.

⬥ Pre-investment safeguards required by the FCA
⬥ Introduced under PS23/6
⬥ Codified across COBS 4.12A and COBS 10A
What These Frictions Ensure
  • › The consumer is suitable (via categorisation)
  • › The product is appropriate (via assessment)
  • › The decision is considered (via delay mechanisms)
1
Cooling-Off Period
COBS 4.12A.18R
▾

A 24-hour pause must follow successful categorisation and assessment. Users cannot trade or invest during this time. The purpose is to encourage reflection and reduce impulsive behaviour.

FCA Requirement
COBS 4.12A.18R: A firm must not communicate a direct-offer financial promotion to a retail client unless at least 24 hours have elapsed since the client first requested to receive such a promotion.
How It Works in Practice
  • The 24 hours begins from the moment a user requests to receive direct-offer financial promotions — typically when they tap 'Sign Up' or 'Get Started' on your platform.
  • During the 24-hour period, users can complete other onboarding steps (KYC, client categorisation, appropriateness assessment) but they must not be able to access any trading functionality.
  • Once the 24 hours has elapsed, the user must be presented with a clear screen offering two equally prominent options: to proceed or to leave the investment journey.
  • A longer cooling-off period is acceptable — the FCA minimum is 24 hours. However, the proceed/leave screen is still required when that period ends.
Automated Message Example
"As part of your onboarding, you are now in a mandatory 24-hour cooling-off period. This gives you time to consider whether cryptoasset trading is right for you. Access to trading will be available from [TIME] on [DATE+1], once all onboarding steps are complete."
UI Suggestion
Include a visible countdown timer or timestamp, showing the user how much time remains.
During Cooling-Off

Your account is almost ready

Before you can start investing, we are required to wait 24 hours from your sign-up.

Time remaining:

18h 32m 14s

We'll notify you when you're ready to invest.

After Cooling-Off (Proceed/Leave)

Your 24-hour period is complete

You can now choose whether to continue with your investment journey.

➔ Continue to Invest
✖ Leave — I don't want to invest
Both options must be equally prominent in size, colour, and placement
Common Mistake to Avoid
The two options ('Continue' and 'Leave') must be displayed with equal visual prominence. One should not appear as a primary button and the other as a greyed-out link. The FCA has cited this as a common failure.

Documentation: Record start/end timestamps and all related communication.

2
Personalised Risk Warning
COBS 4.12A.20R
▾

COBS 4.12A.20R: Deliver individualised risk warnings using the client's full name.

Required Format
"[Client Name], this is a high-risk investment. How would you feel if you lost the money you're about to invest? Take 2 mins to learn more."

Link Requirement: Include a hyperlink with the text "Take 2 mins to learn more", leading to the relevant risk summary (COBS 4.12A.11R(3)).

Delivered during onboarding, prior to client categorisation. Must include prescribed language and apply the user's full name to the message. Must be prominently displayed with the option to continue or leave.

Deliver Option to 'Continue' or 'Leave'

The risk warning must be accompanied by an invitation to the retail client to specify whether they wish to:
(i) leave the investment journey; or
(ii) continue to receive the direct offer financial promotion

Timing
After personal information has been gathered as part of KYC. Presented prior to Client Categorisation and Appropriateness Assessment.
Display Tip
  • › Use bold text, bright colours, or a pop-up to meet prominence standards.
  • › Log client views and acknowledgements for auditing.

Client Categorisations

▾

Ensure clients self-categorise correctly using FCA-mandated investor statements.

Two Options
  • High Net-Worth Investor Statement: Net assets > £250k or income > £100k.
  • Restricted Investor Statement: <10% net assets in high-risk investments.
Links to Prescribed Language
High Net Worth Investor (COBS 4 Annex 2)
Restricted Investor (COBS 4 Annex 5)

4
Appropriateness Assessment
COBS 4.12A.27G / COBS 10 Annex 1
▾
Key Requirements
  • Questions must cover all items listed in COBS 10 Annex 4G.
  • A minimum of 10 questions must be included per assessment.
  • Each set of questions must be unique to prevent repetition across attempts.
  • An 80% pass rate is advised.
  • Do not call this a "quiz"; label it as Appropriateness Assessment.
  • The number of attempts available to a user must equal the number of question banks created.
  • If a user fails all available attempts, they must be informed that cryptoasset investment is not suitable for them and they cannot proceed with onboarding.
  • After a second failed attempt, a user must wait 24 hours before making any further attempts.
Best Practices
  • Integrate into your onboarding UI.
  • Include educational resources for all attempts (whether pass or fail).
  • Enforce increasing cooldowns to protect assessment integrity. E.g. After a user fails twice, they must wait 24 hours before attempting again; after third, 36 hours; fourth, 48 hours.
Compliance Tip
Keep durable records of each assessment attempt.
Example Appropriateness Assessment Questions
Question 1
Which of the following statements about cryptoassets is true?
A. Cryptoassets are backed by tangible assets such as gold or property
B. Cryptoassets are regulated by the UK government and guaranteed to hold value
C. Cryptoassets are high-risk and can lose all their value
D. Investing in cryptoassets is risk-free if done through a recognised exchange
Question 2
What is meant by the term "volatility" in relation to cryptoassets?
A. Prices can change slowly over long periods of time
B. Prices are guaranteed to go up and down in small amounts
C. Prices can rise or fall significantly in a short time
D. Cryptoassets are protected from price swings
Question 3
Which of the following is a risk when holding or trading cryptoassets?
A. Your bank account may be closed
B. You might be taxed on your earnings
C. You could lose access to your assets if you forget your private key or password
D. You will be charged interest on your holdings

Monitoring, Compliance and Reporting Process
Ongoing Obligation
▾

Firms must monitor client behaviour to ensure it aligns with their categorisation.

  • Example: If a user claiming to be a restricted investor engages in high-volume or risky trades, this may trigger re-categorisation protocols.
  • All back-end actions (including friction events) must be logged for compliance.
  • Continuously monitor user trading activity to ensure it aligns with investor category.
  • Maintain and review all onboarding flows and disclosures against latest FCA updates.
  • Keep a detailed compliance log of all onboarding components: categorisations, assessments, cooling-off periods, and risk warnings.
Why It Matters
These non-optional back-end frictions demonstrate that a firm is:
  • › Not onboarding recklessly
  • › Taking steps to educate clients
  • › Preventing harmful behaviour
  • › Compliant with FCA's requirements for cryptoasset promotions

Website Compliance Guide

This guide provides a clear, practical reference for preparing compliant website financial promotions prior to submission. It sets out the core regulatory requirements and common considerations that apply to websites, enabling teams to build these standards into their internal review and sign-off processes. By addressing frequent issues and recurring feedback upfront, this guide aims to improve the quality of initial submissions, reduce avoidable follow-up queries, and support a more efficient and consistent review process.

⬥ Zeyro reviews your entire website — every page accessible to UK users constitutes a financial promotion
⬥ This guide applies to all pages, not just your homepage
⬥ Feedback from the review process will reference the rules set out here

1
The Risk Warning Banner
COBS 4.12A.10R / COBS 4.12A.11R / COBS 4.12A.38G
▾
1.1 Why the Risk Warning Banner Matters

Under COBS 4.12A.10R and COBS 4.12A.11R, every page of your website that constitutes a financial promotion must display the FCA-prescribed risk warning. This is non-negotiable and is the first thing Zeyro checks when reviewing a submission.

1.2 The Prescribed Wording

You must use the following exact wording. It cannot be paraphrased, shortened (unless character limits genuinely prevent it), or have other text inserted within the banner:

Prescribed Wording
"Don't invest unless you're prepared to lose all the money you invest. This is a high-risk investment and you should not expect to be protected if something goes wrong. Take 2 mins to learn more"

Source: COBS 4.12A.11R. This wording must appear verbatim on every page that is a financial promotion.

1.3 The "Take 2 Mins to Learn More" Link

Immediately following the prescribed risk warning, you must include a clickable link with the text "Take 2 mins to learn more" (COBS 4.12A.11R(3)). This link must open your risk summary page — either as a pop-up, a dedicated page on your domain, or an equivalent.

  • The link text must be exactly "Take 2 mins to learn more" — do not rephrase it.
  • The risk summary content must follow the structure set out in COBS 4 Annex 1R.
  • The link must be part of the risk warning banner — not elsewhere on the page.
1.4 Placement — Where the Banner Must Appear

The risk warning banner must be placed at the top of every page below any other fixed elements (such as a navigation bar). It must remain fixed and visible at all times — it must not scroll off screen as the user moves down the page.

  • Apply the banner to every page that a UK user can access, including log-in and registration pages.
  • The banner must stay fixed at the top of the screen when users scroll — this is a common issue we flag.
  • Do not place the risk warning only at the bottom of the page.
1.5 Design and Prominence Requirements

The FCA is very clear that the risk warning must not be diminished by design choices. Under COBS 4.12A.38G and COBS 4.12A.40R, the following design failures will result in rejection:

Design Failures That Will Be Rejected
  • ✗ Using a font size smaller than the standard text size used elsewhere on the page
  • ✗ Using a background colour that does not sufficiently contrast with the text
  • ✗ Making the risk warning dismissible — do not include an 'X' or close button
  • ✗ Fading the text of the risk warning
  • ✗ Placing the risk warning behind an additional click (e.g. requiring users to expand it)
  • ✗ Using a font or background colour that blends with the surrounding content
  • ✗ Allowing the warning to scroll off the screen when the user scrolls down
Real Example From Our Review
One client used black text against a dark grey background for the risk warning. The FCA flagged this directly as a compliance failure. The client also allowed the warning to scroll off screen. Both issues required urgent remediation before approval.
2
The Approval Time Stamp
COBS 4.10.7A
▾
2.1 Why an Approval Time Stamp is Required

Under COBS 4.10.7A, every financial promotion must display, with sufficient prominence, the name of the approving firm (or FRN) and the date on which it was approved.

2.2 The Required Wording
Prescribed Wording
"This Financial Promotion has been approved by Zeyro LTD (FRN 1001386) on [DATE]"
  • Replace [DATE] with the date on which Zeyro provided approval. Do not use a placeholder date or leave the field blank.
  • The stamp must appear on every page that constitutes a financial promotion.
  • It is typically placed at the bottom of each webpage or in the footer — it should be clearly legible.
  • The wording must refer specifically to "financial promotions" — not "offers", "content", or any other term.
2.3 What Happens When You Update Your Website

If you make material changes to your website content after approval, you will need to resubmit the updated pages for review. In some cases (where changes are minor and within the scope of the originally approved content), the original approval date can be retained. Zeyro will confirm this on a case-by-case basis.

Important
If your firm has previously been approved under a different approver name (e.g. Englebert Ltd), please update the approval stamp to reflect "Zeyro LTD (FRN 1001386)" going forward.
3
Balancing Language (Risk Disclaimers)
COBS 4.2.1R
▾
3.1 What Is Balancing Language?

Balancing language refers to risk disclosures that must sit alongside benefits, features, and claims on your website. The FCA requires that promotions give a fair and prominent indication of relevant risks wherever potential benefits are described (COBS 4.2.1R). The prescribed risk warning banner alone is not sufficient — you must also include contextualised risk information throughout the body of your content.

Think of it this way: for every benefit you describe, ask yourself — "What is the corresponding risk that a consumer needs to know?" That risk information needs to appear near the benefit, not buried at the bottom of the page.

3.2 Core Principles
  • Risk disclosures must be presented with equal prominence to the benefits they accompany — matching font size, weight, and visibility.
  • Risk information must not be hidden, de-emphasised, or grouped together at the bottom of a page.
  • Where benefits are described using bold text or large font, the accompanying risk language must also be clearly visible.
  • Risk language must be specific to the feature being described — generic disclaimers are not sufficient.
3.3 Asset-Specific Risks

Where your website refers to specific types of cryptoasset, you must include risk disclosures tailored to that asset type. A general risk warning is not sufficient where specific assets or services are being promoted.

Baseline Risks (All Cryptoassets)
  • Investment risk: The value of cryptoassets can be extremely volatile. You could lose all of the money you invest.
  • Lack of protections: Cryptoassets are largely unregulated. You will not be protected by the FSCS or the FOS if something goes wrong.
  • Selling your investment: You may not always be able to sell your cryptoassets when you want.
  • Cryptoassets are complex: It may not always be clear how a cryptoasset works or what factors influence its value.
  • Diversification: It is good practice not to invest more than 10% of your money in high-risk investments.
Asset-Specific Risk Categories

The following asset types require additional tailored risk disclosures beyond the baseline risks above:

  • DeFi Tokens — Smart contract risk, regulatory risk, rug pulls/exit scams, oracle risk, protocol complexity
  • Memecoins — Extreme volatility, lack of utility, market manipulation, community-driven risks, emotional investing
  • Stablecoins — Counterparty risk, redemption risk, collateral risk, FX risk, algorithmic risk
  • Wrapped Tokens — Smart contract risk, collateral risk, custodial risk, bridging risk, price divergence
  • Security Tokens — Regulatory risk, issuer risk, liquidity risk, legal complexity, custody risk
  • Governance & Utility Tokens — Project dependency, governance concentration, volatility, functionality risk, regulatory uncertainty
3.4 Service/Product Specific Risks

Where your website promotes specific products or services, you must include risk disclosures tailored to that feature.

Service / FeatureRequired Risk Disclosures
StakingPrice volatility while staked; inability to sell during unstaking period; length of lock-up; loss risk — rewards not guaranteed
Crypto LendingCounterparty risk; liquidity risk; smart contract risk; rate variability; collateral risk
Crypto BorrowingLiquidation risk; interest rate risk; collateral volatility; repayment risk; platform risk
OTC TradingCounterparty risk; price risk; liquidity risk; settlement risk; regulatory risk
APY / Yield FiguresRates subject to change and not guaranteed; explain calculation basis; include source and effective date; disclose if promotional
Past Performance ChartsPast performance disclaimer; default to "All Time" / MAX view; include data source
3.5 Placement

Risk disclosures must appear in proximity to the relevant feature or claim — not only in the footer or on a separate page. Where a benefit is promoted in a box, card, or module on your page, the risk disclaimer should be immediately adjacent or directly beneath that element.

✓ Compliant✗ Non-Compliant
✓ "Earn up to 15% APY on select tokens. Rate variable and subject to change. APY is not guaranteed and depends on market conditions. You could lose money." ✗ "Earn up to 15% APY on select tokens." (risk disclaimer buried in footer in small grey text)
✓ Risk language displayed in the same font size and weight as benefit text, directly beneath the feature description. ✗ Risk language displayed in a small font beneath a large headline, making it visually subordinate to the benefit claim.
4
Fair, Clear and Not Misleading
COBS 4.2.1R
▾
4.1 The Core Standard

Under COBS 4.2.1R, every financial promotion must be fair, clear, and not misleading. This standard applies to every piece of content on your website — including headings, straplines, product descriptions, FAQs, and customer journey copy.

4.2 Substantiating Claims

Any claim you make about your product or service must be capable of being evidenced. This includes comparative claims (e.g. "faster", "lower fees"), superlative claims (e.g. "the most cost-effective"), and outcome-based claims (e.g. "save money", "grow your assets").

  • If you cannot evidence a claim, remove or rephrase it.
  • Comparative claims ("faster", "lower") must specify what they are being compared to.
  • Superlative claims ("best", "most", "leading") are high risk — they will almost always require substantiation or removal.
  • Outcome-based claims ("save money", "earn more") imply certainty and must be qualified.
4.3 Common Phrases That Require Attention
Phrase / ClaimSuggested Approach
✗ "Move your money internationally faster and with lower fees"✓ "Move your money internationally quickly and with low fees." (or provide clear, evidenced comparison)
✗ "Enjoy a fast, compliant, and hassle-free platform"✓ Remove 'compliant' unless clearly substantiated. Remove 'hassle-free' as it understates the risks involved.
✗ "[Exchange] is the trusted exchange for XYZ"✓ Either remove or link to verifiable evidence (e.g. Trustpilot rating with a direct link).
✗ "Sign up and start trading immediately"✓ Ensure transparency and reflect the full onboarding process (appropriateness assessment, cooling-off period, etc.).
4.4 Customer Journey Accuracy

Your website must accurately represent the journey a UK user will go through. If your 'How It Works' or 'Get Started' sections present onboarding as simple or immediate, this will be flagged as misleading — because UK users must complete several mandatory steps before they can trade:

  • Self-categorisation (High Net-Worth Investor or Restricted Investor statement)
  • Appropriateness assessment
  • 24-hour cooling-off period
  • Personalised risk warning acknowledgement
Recommendation
If you want to include a 'How It Works' section, frame it as "How [Product] Works Once You Are Set Up" to avoid implying that users can access services immediately.
4.5 Testimonials and Customer Reviews

If your website displays customer testimonials or review ratings (e.g. Trustpilot), these need to be transparent in what they represent. Where they are live (linked to a Trustpilot or equivalent platform), this is generally acceptable. Where they are static elements, they must not contain sensitive words such as "secure", "guaranteed", "trusted", or "protected", and must not be misleading about the nature of the service.

5
Sensitive Words
COBS 4.2.5G
▾
5.1 What Are Sensitive Words?

The FCA has identified certain words as 'sensitive' when used in the context of cryptoasset promotions (COBS 4.2.5G). These words are flagged because they can imply a level of certainty, safety, or protection that is not appropriate given the high-risk, unregulated nature of cryptoassets.

WordWhy It Is ProblematicGuidance
"Secure" / "Securely"Implies capital protection or safety that does not exist for cryptoassetsRemove in most contexts. If referring to a technical process, provide a specific explanation.
"Guaranteed"Implies certainty of outcome — no outcome in cryptoassets can be guaranteedRemove entirely. Do not use in relation to returns, rates, or investment outcomes.
"Protected" / "Safe"Implies FSCS or regulatory protection — none of which applyRemove. If referring to technical safeguards, explain specifically what is and is not protected.
"Easy" / "Simple"Downplays the complexity and risk of cryptoasset investingRemove. Focus on what is well-structured or efficient, with appropriate qualification.
"Compliant"Without substantiation, implies the platform meets all UK regulationsRemove unless clearly substantiated with specific frameworks and a UK-protection disclaimer.
"Hassle-Free"Implies crypto investing is simple and low-effortRemove. Focus on specific, evidenced benefits.
5.2 "Secure" — The Most Common Issue

'Secure' is by far the most frequently flagged word in our website reviews. It appears in many contexts — service descriptions, feature labels, marketing straplines — and in almost all cases it requires amendment.

✓ Compliant✗ Non-Compliant
✓ "We are uncompromising in our approach to security" — describes your attitude, not a guaranteed outcome✗ "Secure crypto payments" — implies capital protection
✓ "Transfers are protected by 256-bit TLS encryption" — explains the specific technical measure✗ "Securely store your crypto assets" — implies capital safety
✓ "KYC and AML checks are conducted via [insert]" — explains a specific process✗ "Launch secure, scalable wallets" — implies the product is protected from risk
5.3 The "Show, Don't Tell" Principle

Rather than labelling your product as 'secure', 'trusted', or 'compliant', explain specifically what processes, safeguards, or standards you apply — and let consumers draw their own conclusions. This approach is both more credible and more compliant.

Example
Instead of "We offer secure digital asset storage", write: "Digital assets are held in cold storage with multi-signature authentication. Please note that cryptoassets are not covered by the FSCS and their value can fall to zero."
6
Regulatory Framing
Claims of Regulatory Status
▾
6.1 The Risk of Misleading Regulatory Language

Many clients are regulated or licensed in other jurisdictions (e.g. as a VASP in the EU). References to this regulatory status on a website directed at UK users can create a false impression that UK regulatory protections apply. The FCA is highly sensitive to this.

Any reference to your regulatory status must be accompanied by a clear clarification that this does not provide UK protections. This clarification must appear with equal prominence to the original claim — not in a footnote or in small text.

6.2 Recommended Clarification Statements
Option A
"We are not a UK-authorised firm, so UK users cannot refer complaints to the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS), and investments are not covered by the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS)."
Option B
"However, this does not provide protections to UK investors."
6.3 Specific Phrases to Avoid or Qualify
  • "Fully regulated and secure" — both words are problematic; remove or qualify with a UK-protection disclaimer.
  • "Licensed VASP + FCA-approved promotions" — misleading; the FCA does not approve promotions. Remove the FCA reference entirely.
  • "Full compliance with financial standards" — unclear which standards; implies UK protection. Specify the framework and disclaim UK protections.
  • "Your assets are protected by institutional-grade custody solutions, regulatory compliance, and insurance for your peace of mind" — this combination is highly misleading for UK users. Rewrite to explain specifically what the custody arrangement covers, with a clear FSCS disclaimer.

1
Risk Warning Banner
COBS 4.12A.10R / COBS 4.12A.38
▾
Risk Warning Statement

As required by COBS 4.12A.10R, all financial promotions must contain the FCA prescribed risk warning:

Prescribed Wording
"Don't invest unless you're prepared to lose all the money you invest. This is a high-risk investment and you should not expect to be protected if something goes wrong. Take 2 mins to learn more"

This banner should be placed at the top of the promotion, and there should be no other text accompanying this statement within the banner.

"Take 2 Minutes to Learn More"

The link to the risk summary should be included here. Where functionality allows, the link should be embedded within the "Take 2 minutes to learn more" call-to-action. For static forms of promotions, a typed-out link is acceptable.

The Banner Itself

As per COBS 4.12A.38, the Financial Promotion must not contain any design feature which has the intent or effect of reducing the visibility or prominence of the risk warning or risk summary.

For app store listings, the ability to interact with font size and colour is limited. Instead it is important here to apply the text to the top of the description section, prior to any other text.

2
Approval Time Stamp
Mandatory Requirement
▾

All UK financial promotions approved by Zeyro must include an approval time stamp, which confirms who approved the promotion and when. The approval time stamp must include Zeyro's FRN and the date of approval, and should be clearly legible within the promotion.

Prescribed Wording
"This Financial Promotion has been approved by Zeyro LTD (FRN 1001386) on [DATE]"
Placement
The approval time stamp should appear at the bottom of the App Store description, clearly legible and not embedded within other text.
3
Balancing Language
Fair, Clear & Not Misleading
▾
Cryptoasset Balancing Language

While the primary risk warning in the Risk Warning banner addresses the general risks of qualifying cryptoassets, additional risk language should be incorporated within the main body of the description to appropriately balance the benefits highlighted across the promotion.

Asset-Specific Risks

Where benefits or features of assets are highlighted, they must be balanced with clear explanations of the associated risks, rather than focusing solely on positive outcomes.

Service-Specific Risks

Specific services will require relevant disclaimers to be added — for example staking, OTC desk, crypto lending, crypto borrowing, etc. Each service that carries distinct risks must have those risks clearly disclosed alongside the benefit claim.

Key Principle
The App Store description must include sufficient balancing language to ensure the promotion is fair, clear and not misleading.
✓ Balanced Approach ✗ Unbalanced Approach
✓ "Earn variable returns on select digital assets. Returns are not guaranteed and are subject to market conditions." ✗ "Earn up to 16% on your crypto!"
✓ "Trade 150+ assets. Cryptoassets are high-risk and prices can fluctuate significantly." ✗ "Trade 150+ assets and build your portfolio!"
✓ "Locked assets may not be immediately accessible and you may be unable to withdraw until the term ends." ✗ "Lock your assets for higher rewards!"
4
Regulatory Framing
Claims of Regulatory Status
▾

References to being regulated or licensed in other jurisdictions can give UK users the impression that the firm or its activities are UK-regulated, or that UK regulatory protections apply. Where such statements are included, clear clarification must also be provided for UK users.

Example Clarification Statement
"We are not a UK-authorised firm, so UK users cannot refer complaints to the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS), and investments are not covered by the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS)."

This clarification should be shown alongside the original claim, and with equal prominence.

Remember
All statements must be fair, clear, and not misleading. A claim of being "licensed in the EU" without UK context could mislead UK users into assuming UK protections apply.
5
Substantiation, Clarity & Sensitive Words
COBS 4.2.5G
▾

All statements must be fair, clear, and not misleading.

Sensitive Words

As per COBS 4.2.5G, the terms "guaranteed", "secure", and "protected" are considered "sensitive words" by the FCA.

If a word diminishes the complexities and risks of cryptoassets, implies certainty and safety of capital, or implies regulatory or compensation protection, then these words should be removed. If being applied to explain systematic processes, security applications etc, then the FCA expects clarity, qualification, and substantiation. If substantiation in each instance cannot be achieved, the phrasing should be revised or removed.

How to Apply
  • › Do not use language that implies certainty of outcomes, risk-free investing, or capital protection
  • › Do not suggest that assets, returns, or user funds are guaranteed
  • › Avoid implying regulatory protection, insurance, or safeguarding unless this is accurate and clearly explained in each instance
✗ Problematic Phrasing ✓ Compliant Alternative
✗ "Your funds are secure" ✓ "We use encryption and multi-factor authentication to help protect your account. However, cryptoassets are not covered by the FSCS."
✗ "Guaranteed returns" ✓ "Variable returns, subject to market conditions. Returns are not guaranteed."
✗ "Protected investments" ✓ "Cryptoassets are high-risk and are not protected by the FSCS."
App Store Visuals
6
Risk Warning in Visuals
Visual Compliance
▾

For visuals that depict the app interface on a mobile device, the prescribed risk warning banner should be included within the phone screen itself, as this reflects how UK users will see the app in practice.

Key Principle
The risk warning must appear as part of the app UI shown in screenshots — not as a separate overlay or footnote outside the device frame.
7
Past Performance in Visuals
Chart Display Requirements
▾
Default Chart View

When App Store Visuals display past performance in the form of a static capture of a live performance chart, the chart display should be defaulted to "All Time" or "MAX". While the in-app functions allow users to change the filter themselves, the default view should not emphasise short-term performance.

Risk Warning

Where past performance charts are displayed, they should be accompanied by a relevant risk warning that highlights that cryptoasset prices are volatile, and that past performance is not indicative of future results.

Common Mistake
Showing a 1-week or 1-month chart where the asset has recently risen sharply. This creates a misleading impression of consistent positive performance. Always default to the longest available time period.
